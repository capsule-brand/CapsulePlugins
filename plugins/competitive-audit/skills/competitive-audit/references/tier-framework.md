# Four-Tier Competitive Landscape

Every credible competitive set fits this taxonomy. Use it to organize the landscape table in §2 of the audit. Pick 5–8 competitors total, distributed roughly 2-2-2-2 (with bias toward whichever tier the client most directly competes in).

## Tier 1 — Direct AI-native rivals

**Who:** Startups with the same wedge, same positioning, often the same pitch deck verbatim. Usually 1–5 years old, often built on modern AI tooling (Lovable, Replit, Bolt). May share the same tagline as the client.

**Threat posture:** Closest direct threat. Will compete on regulation depth, scoring, parent-company distribution, and execution velocity.

**Signals to look for:**
- Same one-line value prop
- Same regulatory frameworks listed
- Similar pricing posture
- Founded recently (post-2023 typical)
- Often Lovable-, Replit-, or Vercel-hosted

**How to research:** Visit their homepage and pricing page via Chrome. Often have thin trust pages.

## Tier 2 — AI-augmented established platforms

**Who:** Mature platforms (5–15 years old) that have bolted AI features onto an existing workflow product. Usually own a broader workflow the client doesn't address — approval routing, version control, brand asset libraries, artwork proofing.

**Threat posture:** Beat the client on enterprise RFPs because procurement wants one tool for the whole workflow. Lose to the client on the SMB self-serve motion because their pricing starts at $1K+/mo.

**Signals to look for:**
- Has both an AI-feature page and a long product page
- Pricing not public, "Talk to sales"
- Customer logos include enterprise brands
- Funded $10M+ or profitable
- Has approval/workflow language ("review and approve", "version history")

**How to research:** Public pricing page, G2/Capterra reviews, comparison pages they've published.

## Tier 3 — Enterprise incumbents

**Who:** Category-defining platforms 15+ years old. Deep data moats (ingredient databases, supplier networks, regulatory intel feeds). Slow to ship AI but trusted in regulated industries. Often sold via long sales cycles to Quality, Reg Affairs, or Procurement.

**Threat posture:** Won't be displaced on price or AI alone. Own the enterprise budget. May ignore the client until they're acquired or pivoted into the category.

**Signals to look for:**
- 90,000+ ingredient database, 1M+ records, etc.
- Five- and six-figure annual contracts
- Multi-year customer relationships
- Have an analyst presence (Gartner, Forrester)
- Acquisitions or rollups visible (e.g., Trustwell = ESHA + FoodLogiQ)

**How to research:** Their own site, analyst quotes, customer case studies, and TrustRadius / G2 for pricing leaks.

## Tier 4 — DIY low-end / adjacent tools

**Who:** Cheap, single-purpose tools that solve one piece of what the client does (or one piece adjacent to it). Often the actual competitor for the smallest customers because the SMB founder conflates the categories.

**Threat posture:** Not a real RFP competitor. But sets the SMB price ceiling buyers anchor against. The client must explain why they're worth 5–10× more than this tier.

**Signals to look for:**
- $25–$300/mo pricing
- Single-purpose (nutrition labels only, recipe-only, etc.)
- Self-serve checkout, no sales motion
- Long-tail of similar tools (10+ options in this tier)

**How to research:** Pricing page and one G2 review.

## Tiering edge cases

**Indirect competitors / substitutes:** A consultant or agency doing the job manually is often the de-facto competitor. Mention briefly in Tier 4 commentary; don't profile separately.

**Adjacent expansion threats:** A company in a neighboring category that could enter (e.g., a regulatory-intel platform that could add label review). Note in the strategic posture column of the landscape table; don't include in the head-to-head comparisons.

**Same-company multiple products:** If a parent has two products in scope (e.g., GoVisually's main proofing tool + Label Score AI), profile the most-direct one and mention the other in passing.

## Landscape table template

The §2 table in the audit follows this format:

| Tier | Players | Pricing range | Strategic posture vs. {client} |
|---|---|---|---|

Keep the strategic posture column substantive — one sentence on how this tier competes, not a generic label.
